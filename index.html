What to Do When You Feel Overwhelmed: A Practical Guide

Feeling overwhelmed is something almost everyone experiences. It's that sense of being pulled in too many directions, with too many demands and not enough time or energy to meet them all.

Simphiwe Mtetwa Host
28 minutes
0:00 / 28:00

About This Episode

Feeling overwhelmed is something almost everyone experiences. It's that sense of being pulled in too many directions, with too many demands and not enough time or energy to meet them all.

In this episode, we explore practical strategies for managing overwhelm, from prioritization techniques to mindfulness practices that can help you regain control and find clarity in chaotic situations.

Whether you're a busy professional, a student juggling multiple responsibilities, or simply someone feeling the weight of modern life's demands, this guide offers actionable insights to help you navigate overwhelm with grace and purpose.

Key Takeaways

  • Understanding the root causes of overwhelm
  • Practical prioritization frameworks
  • Mindfulness techniques for immediate relief
  • Building sustainable habits for long-term wellbeing

Episode Transcript

[00:00] Welcome back to the podcast. Today we're tackling something that affects almost everyone at some point - that feeling of being completely overwhelmed.

[00:15] You know the feeling. You have a dozen tasks demanding your attention, your phone won't stop buzzing, and somehow you're supposed to be productive through it all.

[00:30] But here's the thing - overwhelm isn't a sign of weakness. It's a signal. And today, we're going to explore what that signal is telling us and, more importantly, what we can do about it.

[01:00] Let's start by understanding what overwhelm actually is. It's not just having a lot to do. It's that specific feeling where your capacity to handle things feels exceeded by the demands placed upon you.

[02:00] The first step in managing overwhelm is recognizing it. Many of us push through, thinking we just need to work harder. But that often makes things worse.

[03:00] So what can we do? The first strategy is something I call the "triage method" - assessing what actually needs your immediate attention versus what can wait.

Why Inequality Persists: Slaves Made a Deal with Their Masters and The Children of The Slaves Continue to uphold the Masters’ Deal

Simphiwe Mtetwa
April 15, 2026 • 6 min read

There is a detail from South Africa’s corporate history that deserves far greater attention than it receives — and it belongs, fittingly, at the beginning of this story. In the mid-1980s, as apartheid entered its final phase and pressure mounted both domestically and internationally, Anglo-American, the most powerful mining conglomerate in the country at the time, did not simply wait for history to unfold. It prepared for it. Under the leadership of its chairman Gavin Relly, and driven by executive Clem Sunter, the company developed a series of scenario-planning models that would later become widely known as the “High Road” and the “Low Road”. The Low Road envisioned a future in which minority rule clung to power, plunging the country into conflict and economic collapse. The High Road imagined a negotiated transition to democracy and South Africa’s reintegration into the global economy.

These were not abstract exercises. Sunter presented these scenarios to the cabinet of F. W. de Klerk and engaged, shortly before his release, with Nelson Mandela. By the early 1990s, the Montfleur Scenario Exercise had expanded this work, bringing together political leaders, business figures, trade unionists and intellectuals to map the country’s possible futures. Business, in other words, had done its arithmetic long before the negotiations began. It had examined the full range of outcomes, calculated what would need to be conceded to preserve the system, and quietly determined that what it was willing to offer would be only a fraction of what it possessed. That is the context—deliberate, calculated, and historically documented—in which one must understand a striking insight from Steven Friedman.

"Slaves made a deal with their masters!"

It is an image that is difficult to shake: think about it, slaves seated at a table, negotiating with their masters. The force of that idea became clearer to me through an entirely ordinary, domestic experience. Recently, I had to carry out some maintenance on my house. There were cracked tiles, peeling paint, and a roof beginning to yield to the Johannesburg rains. I sat down and worked through the costs — tiles, waterproofing, sealant, labour — and arrived at a figure of roughly R10,000. Enough to see the house safely through winter. But there was something I did not say aloud at the time. I could afford R10,000 precisely because I had considerably more than R10,000. My willingness to spend that amount rested on a quiet certainty: that doing so would not destabilise me. The apparent generosity of the gesture — if it can even be called that — was entirely relative to what remained unseen.

That small moment stayed with me long after the last tile had been laid. This moment serves, in its own quiet way, as a precise metaphor for how inequality in South Africa was constructed—and why, thirty years into democracy, it persists. As liberation became inevitable, those who controlled economic power did not simply yield. They prepared. They calculated, with care, how much they were willing to place on the table—and they ensured that what they offered would not threaten what they intended to keep. Those who came to sit across from them arrived from an entirely different position. They had been worn down by decades of deliberate exclusion. Many were encountering, for the first time, the full material weight of what had long been denied to them. The table was not merely inviting; it was overwhelming. So, understandably, the instinct was to sit at the table and partake in what had always been withheld.

This is not a moral judgement on those who negotiated the transition. It is an observation about power, context and human psychology. When a door has been closed to you for a lifetime, your first impulse when it opens is not to question the architecture of the room. It is to step inside. Yet the room — and the table within it — had already been designed. This is the foundation of Friedman’s central argument: that South Africa is divided not simply by race or class, but by a more enduring distinction between insiders and outsiders. Insiders are those connected to the formal economy – earning, investing, and accumulating. Outsiders remain structurally excluded from these systems.

Under apartheid, this divide was explicitly racial. Today, it is formally economic, though still deeply shaped by the racial architecture of the past. What is striking is not only that the divide persists but also that the original settlement produced a particular kind of insider class — one that inherited access to the economy but not necessarily the freedom to reimagine it. The children of that transition now manage what was conceded, often guided by a psychological inheritance shaped by scarcity, exclusion and the urgency to secure what had previously been denied.

Human development research consistently shows that early childhood — those formative years between birth and roughly seven — leaves a lasting imprint. Values, anxieties and assumptions about the world are absorbed long before they are consciously examined. A child raised in an environment defined by exclusion and precarity does not simply shed those conditions when circumstances improve. The imprint remains, often subtly shaping decisions about risk, wealth and belonging. This is part of the reason inequality proves so stubborn. It is not sustained by policies alone but by deeply embedded patterns of thought and behaviour.

It is reinforced, too, by the values that dominate public life. Worth continues to be measured in terms of wealth, consumption and visible success. The priorities of society reflect the interests of insiders. When water shortages affect suburban households, the response is swift and urgent. When similar or worse conditions persist in townships and rural areas, they often receive less attention. Even the proposed solutions often reproduce the problem. There is near-universal agreement that formal employment is the primary path out of inequality. Yet formal employment is shrinking globally, including in South Africa. Meanwhile, millions sustain themselves through informal economic activity that is dynamic, adaptive and resilient.

Too often, corporate social investment programmes are designed to “formalise” these livelihoods, rather than to support and strengthen them on their own terms. Similarly, social grants are frequently dismissed as fostering dependency. Yet evidence consistently shows that they stimulate local economies and enable recipients to pursue additional sources of income. Properly understood, they are not a burden but a foundation. What Friedman ultimately calls for is not incremental reform, but a shift in imagination — a willingness to confront the structural limits embedded in the original settlement.

Those who came to the negotiating table with power understood something fundamental: you can only give away what you are prepared to lose. What was offered was carefully calibrated. It was enough to secure transition, but not enough to fundamentally redistribute power. Thirty years later, South Africa remains shaped by that calculation. The challenge now is not simply to do more within the existing framework but to question the framework itself. The corporate social investment sector, along with anyone committed to dismantling inequality, faces the task of not only increasing outputs but also expanding participation, reducing exclusion, and deliberately reshaping the underlying architecture of the economy. Because, in the end, the work required is not cosmetic. The renovation goes far deeper than the tiles.

Simphiwe Mtetwa

Driving impact, enabling progress, and serving our communities with integrity.

By Simphiwe Mtetwa
Date 13 Apr 2026
Topic Editor's View, CSI Indaba

This closed session brings together confirmed and conditional speakers for the CSI Indaba, and marks a critical step in shaping the depth, direction and intellectual integrity of what we are building.

From the outset, we have been intentional about what the CSI Indaba must be. Not simply another convening, but a platform that delivers clear value, sharp thinking, and meaningful contribution to the sector. The Intellectual Foundation sessions are a key part of that intent—ensuring that what is presented on stage aligns with what our delegates truly need from this space.

This does not exist in isolation. South Africa is home to many exceptional platforms advancing the CSI agenda, and we acknowledge the role they continue to play in driving growth and transformation across the sector. We have learned from them, and we respect the ground they have laid. At the same time, we are building something distinct.

I would like to extend my sincere thanks to every individual who submitted a speaker proposal earlier this year. Each 600-word submission represented not only an idea, but a commitment to shaping the future of CSI. We read every single one—personally, I read nearly all of them in full. That process was important to us. It was about recognising your voice, your thinking, and your contribution to this industry.

  • To those selected as speakers congratulations.
  • To those in the conditional category congratulations.
  • And to those not selected this year this is not a reflection of your work, but rather a reflection of a very deliberate and focused first-year agenda.
Because the CSI Indaba is not a once-off event. It is a 25-year vision.

What you are beginning to see now—monthly webinars, ongoing podcast conversations, curated engagements—is the start of a continuum. A build-up that is responsive to the sector, aligned to your needs, and designed to deepen the quality of discourse long before we gather in person.

We are also seeing new audiences emerge, alongside a broader and more diverse set of partners joining this journey. That, in itself, is a powerful signal of what is possible. Tickets are now at full price, and I encourage you to secure your place. Those who join us early—particularly in April—will gain access to our full webinar series at no additional cost. These sessions are not supplementary; they are foundational to the Indaba experience.

In the coming months, we will be joined by voices such as clinical psychologist Nomfundo Mogapi, among others, as we continue to build towards a programme that is both rigorous and relevant. As you consider your participation, I encourage you to be deliberate. Be clear about what you seek to gain, and equally clear about what you intend to contribute.

Because ultimately, our collective responsibility is simple:

to drive impact, enable progress, and serve our communities with integrity—while ensuring that the work we do is recognised, valued, and properly compensated. This is the work. And this is just the beginning.

For decades, Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) has been treated as a peripheral function — a department tucked away between marketing and compliance, tasked with balancing the scales of corporate conscience. But as we move deeper into the 21st century, it is becoming increasingly clear that the old model of charitable giving and transactional philanthropy is no longer enough. The world does not need more "projects"; it needs systemic change.

Systemic change is about looking at the roots of social challenges rather than just the symptoms. It’s about asking why a community remains impoverished despite decades of aid, and then redesigning the economic and social structures that keep those barriers in place. It requires moving from a mindset of "giving back" to one of "building forward."

The Myth of Compliance

Many organizations fall into the trap of viewing CSR through the lens of compliance. They meet their B-BBEE requirements, file their reports, and consider their duty done. But compliance is a floor, not a ceiling. When we treat social responsibility as a box-ticking exercise, we strip it of its transformative potential. True leadership in this space requires a willingness to exceed mandates and seek out genuine impact.

"The future belongs to those who build it today, not those who merely report on it."

Simphiwe Mtetwa

The Architecture of Impact

Driving systemic change requires a new architecture of impact. This involves three critical shifts in corporate thinking:

1. From Transactional to Transformational: Instead of one-off donations, companies should invest in long-term partnerships that build capacity within communities. This means providing not just funds, but expertise, technology, and access to markets.

2. From Silos to Ecosystems: No single company can solve a systemic issue alone. We must learn to work within ecosystems, partnering with government, NGOs, and even competitors to address shared challenges at scale.

3. From External to Integrated: Social responsibility cannot be separate from the core business strategy. It must be integrated into every decision, from supply chain management to product development. When impact is part of the business model, it becomes sustainable and scalable.

The work we are doing at the CSI Indaba is a testament to this shift. We are not just convening a meeting; we are building an intellectual foundation for a 25-year vision. This is the work of our generation — to move beyond the checklist and begin the hard, necessary work of driving change that lasts.

About This Episode

In the latest episode of The Future of Corporate Social Investment (CSI), Lunga Schoeman, a prominent leader in the CSI space, shares his vision for how corporate investment can drive meaningful, measurable change across South Africa.

Lunga explores the critical shift from traditional corporate philanthropy — writing cheques and walking away — to a new model of strategic, long-term investment that builds capacity within communities and creates sustainable impact.

Drawing on decades of experience across the public and private sectors, Lunga unpacks the frameworks that are reshaping how companies think about their social license to operate, and why the old models no longer serve the communities they claim to support.

Key Takeaways

  • Why CSI must move beyond charity to strategic investment
  • Building genuine partnerships with communities
  • Measuring what matters: impact over output
  • The role of leadership in driving systemic change
